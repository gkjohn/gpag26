I.Preface

“We came into wealth we did not plan for. We realised, early, that it did not fully belong to us. A country of 1.4 billion people cannot be held together by government and markets alone. It needs something older and harder to measure: a thriving civil society. For almost thirty years, we have tried to help build that. Not as a project. As a practice.”

Rohini & Nandan Nilekani · First generation · Giving since 1997
II.A framework, in three words

What a good society requires.
Three estates. Each doing what only it can do.

SamaajSociety
The first estate. Where trust is made and meaning is held. Relationships, dignity, civic life.
SarkaarState
The second estate. Mandate, law, the public commitment. Builds for the whole.
BazaarMarket
The third estate. Exchange, enterprise, signal. Builds for margin and motion.

A good society emerges when each does what only it can do — and trusts the others enough to let them. Our work has lived across all three. Separately. Together. In the spaces between.

III.Two Questions · Thirty Years · One Country

Two questions.
Thirty years. One country.

They lived inside the same household and asked different things of it. The exhibit that follows is the conversation between those questions — and what each, eventually, required of the other.

Rohini asked
What makes a system worth building?

She began with people, not platforms. With reading rooms, riverbeds, the inside of a clinic at 6 a.m. She asked whether the institutions we build can hold the dignity of those who live inside them.

Nandan asked
How do you build systems that work for everyone?

He began with architecture. With the rails beneath an economy. He asked whether public infrastructure could be built once, openly, so that a billion people could build on top of it without asking permission.

VI. The bridge — public goods

How do you give
in a way that compounds?

A country of 1.4 billion. Even the largest cheque, deployed as a service, reaches a rounding error.

“Fund the rails. Not the services.”

Markets build for margin. Governments build for mandate. Protocols come years before scale — and almost only philanthropy can fund them. Patient enough to be wrong. Public enough to be useful when it works.

VIII.Closing

Systems without society
become extractive.
Society without systems

What this exhibit is, in the end

Not a record of giving. An argument about what a society requires of itself. The final note is not triumph. It is responsibility — the kind that compounds quietly, over decades, in the spaces between estates.

Where to begin

Fund the rails. Or fund the people who tend them. Pick the hard problem you can stay with for ten years. Then join a pool — and let the work be larger than any one cheque.
